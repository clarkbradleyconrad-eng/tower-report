Summer Portal Window Opens: What Texas Is Targeting at OL

With the interior line still unsettled, Texas has entered the summer portal window with a clear focus: experienced guards who can step in and compete for a starting role immediately.

The transfer portal opened its summer window on June 1. For most programs, it's an opportunity to add depth. For Texas in 2026, it's something more specific: the search for the experienced interior offensive lineman who changes the calculus of the entire season.

The situation is well-documented by this point. Trevor Goosby at left tackle and Connor Robertson at center provide genuine anchors. But two guard spots — left and right — enter fall camp without a clear, experienced starter. That's the hole Sarkisian has been candid about, and the portal is one of the mechanisms he's using to address it.

Who's Already Here

Worth noting first: Laurence Seymore and Paris Patterson Jr. — two OL transfers who signed in the spring — are already in Austin. They weren't included on the post-spring depth chart (per the On Texas Football staff, which noted summer additions would be incorporated before preseason camp), but they are on campus, learning the system, and will be evaluated throughout the summer workout program.

Both bring SEC-caliber experience. Seymore, an interior lineman from a conference program, has the physical profile Texas is looking for — long arms, base strength, and the kind of football intelligence that allows him to absorb a new system in condensed timelines. Patterson Jr. is a more projectable player with elite athleticism but a shorter track record of extended starts. Either or both could resolve the guard question before a ball is snapped in fall camp.

What the Staff Is Looking For

In conversations with sources familiar with the staff's evaluation process, a clear profile emerges for the portal target Texas is prioritizing. The ideal addition is an experienced Power 4 guard who has started at least one full season, has operated in a zone-blocking or outside-zone system, and is physically mature enough to handle SEC defensive tackles on Day 1.

That last criterion is the difficult one. A guard from a Group of Five program might have starting experience and good technique, but the physical gap between Conference USA defensive tackles and the ones Texas will face in SEC play — Ohio State's interior, LSU's, Texas A&M's — is significant. The staff wants someone who has already been in a comparable physical environment.

The psychological fit matters too. Coming into Texas in June, learning a new system over eight weeks before the season opener, and being expected to start against college football's premier competition requires a specific mentality. Sarkisian has been explicit: the standard for a portal addition at this position is higher than for depth additions, because the expectation is immediate contribution in high-stakes environments.

"If the right player is available and he fits what we're doing, we'll take him. If he doesn't fit, we trust the competition already in the building. We're not adding bodies — we're adding starters."
Steve Sarkisian — Spring Press Conference
The Timeline

The summer portal window closes in mid-July. After that, any addition would need to go through the fall evaluation process — technically possible but practically complex. The staff's preference is to have the roster settled before August 1, when preseason camp begins and reps become the currency everyone is competing for.

That gives Texas approximately six weeks from the portal opening to identify, evaluate, offer, and secure a commitment from the player who resolves the guard question. Six weeks in the modern portal era is actually a comfortable timeline — most decisions move in 72 hours once both sides are aligned.

The leverage Texas brings to this search is significant. The program's brand, its offensive system's reputation for developing linemen for the NFL, its schedule that features genuine marquee games, and Sarkisian's reputation as an offensive mind who maximizes OL development — all of these are real recruiting assets in a portal market where most linemen are weighing competitive starting opportunities carefully.

If the Portal Doesn't Deliver

The fallback scenario is not catastrophic. Jaydon Chatman and Brandon Baker are both capable players who could grow into the role. The spring depth chart suggests internal competition is genuine — multiple players are pushing for the same spots, which is healthy. The question is whether internal growth happens fast enough to meet the Week 2 standard that Ohio State demands.

Sarkisian's internal solution timeline: both guards need to be at or near their ceiling by September 12. That's the date by which the offensive line will be evaluated by 102,000 people in Columbus — or rather, by Ohio State's defensive front — and found sufficient or found wanting. Everything in the next ten weeks is building toward that specific test.

Bottom Line
The summer portal window is open and Texas is actively working it at offensive guard. Adding even one proven, experienced guard from a Power 4 program changes this roster's ceiling materially. The staff has been selective and will remain so — they're not adding bodies to fill depth chart slots, they're looking for a starter who can handle the SEC's best defensive fronts from Day 1. The resolution arrives before preseason camp, one way or another. The answer to whether Texas's OL question is solved will be known in early August.
