5-Star DE Sets Official Visit to Austin for July Fourth Weekend

Marcus Johnson is 6-foot-5, 245 pounds, from Katy, Texas — and he may be the most important football recruit in the state of Texas for 2027. The nation's #3 edge rusher on 247Sports and ESPN, Johnson is the kind of transformational prospect that programs build defensive generations around. Think of what Simmons is doing for Texas's 2026 defense. Johnson, in 2027, would be the continuation of that legacy.

On Thursday, Johnson's family confirmed through his high school coach that the edge rusher would make an official visit to Austin for the July Fourth holiday weekend. The visit had been anticipated for weeks — Texas has made multiple in-home visits, and the Longhorns staff has invested significant time and resources in this recruitment. The official visit confirms Texas is still very much in the running for a prospect who has drawn interest from nearly every elite program in the country.

Who Marcus Johnson Is

Johnson plays at Katy Taylor High School in Katy, Texas, one of the top football factories in the state. At 6'5" and 245 pounds as a rising junior, he is physically ahead of where most elite edge rushers are at his stage. His first step is elite. His hand technique is already sophisticated — he uses a long-arm/spin combination to beat college-level blockers in 7-on-7 settings. His motor doesn't stop on any play.

The scouting consensus is clear: Johnson is a Top-5 national player in the 2027 class, regardless of position. His upside — the combination of frame, athleticism, and already-advanced technique — is elite even by the standards of a class that already features several potential top-10 NFL Draft picks of the future. The team that signs Marcus Johnson is adding a foundational piece to their defense for the next four seasons.

The Simmons Effect

Colin Simmons has changed something real about how edge rushers evaluate Texas. Before Simmons, the pitch for a pass rusher at UT was straightforward: great program, good development, SEC competition. After Simmons's 2025 season — 11 sacks, the EA CF26 cover, the Bednarik Award conversation — the pitch has an additional dimension: Texas is where great pass rushers go to become elite.

The Simmons model is specific and powerful for recruiting purposes. Here is a player who came to Texas as the nation's top prospect, worked in Pete Kwiatkowski's system for two years, and emerged as the most dominant defensive player in college football. The message to Johnson is implicit but unmistakable: this is what the path looks like here. This is what the development looks like. If you want to go where the best pass rushers go, the answer is Austin.

Johnson's family has cited Simmons's development multiple times in conversations with the staff. It's not the only factor — the education, the NIL landscape, the opportunity to play early and for a program with CFP ambitions all matter. But the Simmons halo effect is real, and it's a factor that Texas simply did not have in previous recruiting cycles.

"When you see what happened with Simmons — the development, the game, what he's building toward — it makes you want to be in that room. Texas is building something at that position that's generational."

The Competition

Georgia and Ohio State are the legitimate competitors at this stage of the recruitment. Georgia's defensive program needs no introduction — their NFL Draft production at every defensive position remains the gold standard nationally, and their pitch to Johnson centers on joining the most respected defensive tradition in the sport.

Ohio State's pitch is different: their developing relationship with Johnson's high school coach, their proximity to NFL markets in the Midwest, and Ryan Day's recent success with defensive recruiting after hiring a new DC. It's a credible offer from a credible program.

But Texas's advantages in this specific recruitment are significant. The in-state factor is real — Johnson is from the Houston area, and all recruiting services cite his family's preference to stay in Texas as a genuine consideration. The Simmons factor has been discussed. And the July 4th visit, in Austin, with the program's off-season buzz at a high point, is the kind of experience that seals commitments from in-state prospects who are leaning toward home.

What a Commitment Would Mean

Texas already has three commitments in the 2027 class, all on defense. Adding Johnson would give the Longhorns an elite edge rusher who could eventually step into the role Simmons has built — not a replacement for Simmons in any way, but a continuation of the defensive identity that's defining the Sarkisian era.

The visit happens July 3-5. The coaching staff will have Simmons available — presumably home for the holiday — to talk with Johnson directly. If Texas closes during or shortly after this visit, the 2027 recruiting class begins to take shape around a defensive foundation that could be the most talented in program history.

Bottom Line
Marcus Johnson's official visit to Austin is the most important recruiting event of Texas's summer. The 247Sports Crystal Ball currently favors Texas at 70%, and the Longhorns' combination of in-state appeal, Simmons's development proof-of-concept, and the specific pitch of Pete Kwiatkowski's system makes this recruitment genuine. A commitment in July would change the trajectory of the 2027 class and continue the defensive dynasty that Simmons started. Texas is the team to beat. The visit will tell us how hard the close needs to be.
