Texas's Offensive Line Is the One Question Nobody Wants to Answer

With elite skill positions and a dominant defense, it's tempting to look past the interior line. That's a mistake. Here's the honest depth chart reality heading into fall camp.

The 2026 Texas offensive line enters fall camp with genuine strength at the edges and genuine questions in the interior. Two guard spots, a retooled right side, and a schedule that features some of the best defensive fronts in college football create the central challenge of Sarkisian's sixth season.

LT Trevor Goosby and C Connor Robertson are proven anchors — the bookends of stability on this line.
Two interior guard spots remain unsettled entering fall camp with multiple players in competition.
Ohio State (W2), Tennessee (W4), and LSU (W10) all have elite pass-rush units capable of exposing weaknesses.
The OL is the single biggest swing variable in whether this Texas team goes 11–1 or 9–3.

Everyone wants to talk about Arch Manning's arm. Everyone wants to talk about Colin Simmons's sack totals. Everyone wants to talk about Cam Coleman's speed and Ryan Wingo's route-running and the four-receiver sets that are going to make this Texas offense genuinely terrifying.

Nobody wants to talk about the offensive line. And that's exactly the problem.

The 2026 Texas Longhorns have a national championship ceiling. They also have an offensive line that, in its current form, has two genuinely unsettled interior spots heading into fall camp. On a schedule that includes Ohio State in Week 2, Tennessee on the road in Week 4, LSU at Death Valley in Week 10, and Texas A&M at Kyle Field on Thanksgiving — two unsettled guard spots are not a detail. They are the story.

What Texas Has

Start with what's real. Trevor Goosby at left tackle is a genuine strength. The senior is one of the best pass-protecting left tackles in the SEC — long, technically sound, and experienced against elite edge rushers. His performance against Ohio State's Dawand Jones in 2025 was mixed, but he held up. Against Simmons's pass-rush counterparts on the 2026 schedule, Goosby is as capable as any LT in the conference.

At center, Connor Robertson is the steady hand this offense needs. A fifth-year senior who has been in Sarkisian's system long enough to make the presnap calls that keep everything organized. Robertson's value goes beyond the snap count — he's the interior intelligence, the player who communicates line assignments to guards who may be in their first major role.

At right tackle, Melvin Siani returns with experience and quickness. He's not Goosby, but he's a reliable SEC tackle who won't be a liability against most opponents.

The edge of this offensive line — Goosby, Robertson, Siani — is a legitimate strength. That's a foundation. The problem is everything in between.

The Interior Problem

Left guard and right guard enter fall camp as open competitions. Sarkisian has been direct about it: the staff will let the best players compete. What he hasn't said publicly — and what the roster reflects — is that there isn't an obvious, experienced answer at either position.

Jaydon Chatman is the most likely LG starter entering camp. A junior with athleticism but limited starts, Chatman has the tools to develop into a solid SEC guard. The question is whether he gets there by Week 1 against Texas State or whether the real development happens under fire against Ohio State in Week 2.

At right guard, Brandon Baker is the veteran option — experienced and physical in the run game, but with questions about his ability to handle speed rushers at the highest level. Devin Coleman will push for the spot throughout camp.

What the Schedule Demands

Here's the cold reality: the 2026 schedule is designed to expose an unsettled interior offensive line. Ohio State's defensive front finished 2025 ranked among the top five nationally in sacks. Tennessee's edge unit is built around speed and disruption. LSU has one of the most physically dominant defensive lines in the SEC. Texas A&M's front seven has been their calling card for three straight seasons.

Arch Manning can compensate for some OL deficiency with quick release, mobility, and the ability to move the pocket. He did it at moments in 2025 when protection broke down. But sustained pressure — the kind that comes from interior penetration — is different from edge pressure. When guards are getting beaten at the point of attack, there's no escape.

The run game compounds the issue. Texas wants to establish Raleek Brown and Hollywood Smothers as a genuine ground threat in 2026 — both to control tempo and to reduce the pressure on Manning in hostile road environments. But a run game only works with interior blocking that can win at the point of attack. Two unsettled guards make that a genuine concern.

What Has to Happen

The staff's path is clear. Fall camp must produce two functional, experienced guards before Week 1. The Texas State opener in Week 1 is a tune-up — Sark will use it to evaluate guard play under real game conditions before the calendar turns to Ohio State. That evaluation window is narrow.

The other variable is the portal. As the summer portal window opens, Texas has been connected to multiple experienced interior linemen. If the staff finds the right fit — an experienced starter from a Power 4 program who understands zone blocking and can step in immediately — the problem changes significantly. The standard is clear: experienced, physical, and scheme-intelligent enough to handle Robertson's presnap calls from day one.

There's a version of 2026 where the guard spots get sorted, Goosby has his best season, and the OL is a genuine strength. That version produces ten wins minimum and a real CFP run. There's another version where the guards never get settled, pressure accumulates in key spots on the schedule, and Manning is running for his life in Columbus and Knoxville.

The talent in every other room on this roster is good enough to absorb almost any deficiency. The offensive line is the one room where the margin for error is zero.

Bottom Line
Texas's offensive line is not a fatal weakness. It is the program's most significant question mark entering 2026. Two unsettled guard spots on a schedule loaded with elite defensive fronts creates a swing variable that no amount of Arch Manning talent can fully compensate for. Solve the interior in fall camp and this team has a national championship ceiling. Don't solve it, and three losses become very possible. The next three months are the most important in the program since Sarkisian arrived.
