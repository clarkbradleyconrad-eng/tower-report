What the Numbers Actually Say

In 2025, Anthony Hill Jr. recorded 94 total tackles, 62 of them solo. He added 8 tackles for loss, 3 sacks, 4 pass breakups, and 2 interceptions. His team was 10-3. He played in 13 games. He covered more ground on more snaps than any other player on this defense.

Those numbers are good. In a normal defensive environment, they would be the conversation. But the number that matters most doesn't appear in the box score: Texas was 9-1 in games where Hill recorded 8 or more tackles. That's not correlation. That's causation. When Hill is making plays, the entire defensive structure functions. When he doesn't — two of the three losses tell that story.

The PFF grade was 87.6 — that placed him in the top 10 linebackers nationally. The coverage grade alone (81.4) was remarkable for a player who also recorded 3 sacks. It's the combination that makes him unique: a linebacker who can rush the passer, cover tight ends in man coverage, and still make 7 tackles a game.

The Intelligence Layer Nobody Talks About

Here's what matters most about Anthony Hill Jr. and it has nothing to do with his own statistics: Hill is the presnap communicator for Texas's defense. He reads the offensive formation, identifies the strength, makes the call, and adjusts the entire defensive structure before the ball is snapped. Connor Robertson does it for the offense. Hill does it for the defense.

That matters enormously when you have a player like Simmons whose entire value proposition is aggression and first-step explosion. Simmons is almost impossible to slow down when he knows exactly where he's going on the snap count. The reason he knows — the reason the entire defense knows — is because Hill has processed the presnap information and communicated the right assignment.
